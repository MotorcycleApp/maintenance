Battery

The battery can be attributed to many cases of failure-to-start each spring, but there are a few options to salvage what charge remains. First, connect the battery to a tender or trickle charger and fill it up with electrical juice. If that doesn’t do it, you’ll have to jump the battery, though it’s not the best idea on a small motorcycle battery (this is a worst-case scenario). And, finally, if all else fails, head on down to your dealer and order a new battery. You can avoid most battery mishaps by removing your battery and storing it inside when the bike is not in use for long durations, or hooking it up to a battery tender in the garage.

Air filter

If you know how to get to your airbox, go on and pop it open. What do you see? A smiling, happy, clean, and dapper air filter? Or do you see a grimy, nasty, smelly, pig of an air filter? If it’s the latter, you know what to do. Some filters can be cleaned and re-oiled like K&Ns, so get to scrubbing. Otherwise, you’ll have to replace it.

Fuel system

Let’s talk about fuel. It’s certainly not getting any cheaper, and it’s becoming less motorcycle-friendly, thanks to increases in ethanol integration. Ethanol is evil corn alcohol that causes gasoline to gum up fuel systems from the tank and all the way to the carburetors. That’s why it’s so important to drain your carburetors before you store your bike for the winter and use a fuel stabilizer for the gas that’s already in the tank. If you have neglected to accomplish either one of these imperative tasks, then your fuel system might be stuffy. If your bike is running rough and stumbly, it’s most likely the carburetors or injectors, and they need to be cleaned. Cleaning carbs or injectors is a bit of a challenge at first, but it can turn into a simple routine with enough practice. Regardless, take it to the dealer if you’re not comfortable digging into a very important area on your bike.

Valves

The last major arena we’re going to investigate is well-hidden. The valves on a motorcycle are one of the most important areas and need to be adjusted in periodic mileage increments. Check your mileage on the bike and refer to the valve adjustment schedule. If you’re due for a valve adjustment, now is the time to take care of it. Schedule a date with a dealer, or invite your mechanic neighbor over and offer him a 12-pack and a pizza in exchange for his services. While poking around the engine, be sure to take a look at all of the gaskets, and make sure everything looks OK with the headers and exhaust system.

Major bolt torque specs

Lastly, whip out that manual and check the torque of your bike’s major bolts, using a click-type torque wrench for higher torque values or a beam-type torque wrench for more delicate torque values. We’re talking swingarm pivot, axles, handlebars, calipers, steering stem, and so on. This will prevent any treacherous rattling sounds that could lead to something catastrophic on the road.

Hit the road

Now it’s safe to ride. We know portions of this guide seemed as though they were steeped in OCD, but it is uncanny how many accidents transpire within the first week of riding season. Make sure your ride is 100% roadworthy before even thinking about throwing your girl on the back, let alone yourself. The other thing to consider is sand in the road and other road hazards summoned by winter’s finest. You’ve had a five- to six-month vacation from riding, so throttle it back and allot ample time to re-acquaint yourself with your motorcycle. Ride safe!
